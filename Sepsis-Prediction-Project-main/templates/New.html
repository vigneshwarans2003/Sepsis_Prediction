Sepsis - Frequently Asked Questions

Common Signs of Sepsis

▼
  • Fever
  • Fast heart rate
  • Rapid breathing
  • Confusion
  • Body pain

It can lead to septic shock, multiple organ failure, and death.

Cause of Sepsis

▼

Bacterial infections cause most cases of sepsis. However, viral infections, such as COVID-19 or influenza; fungal infections; or noninfectious insults.

Organs Responsible

▼

Those that more commonly cause sepsis include infections of:

  • Lungs, such as pneumonia
  • Kidney, bladder, and other parts of the urinary system
  • Digestive system

Prevention From Sepsis

▼

Sepsis prevention is only possible by preventing infections with good and consistent hygiene and avoiding people with infections.

Solution Vaccine

▼

The MenACWY vaccine is for meningitis, sepsis, and septicaemia (blood poisoning), which are very serious, potentially life-threatening illnesses that can lead to life-changing disabilities, such as amputations, hearing loss, and brain damage.

Immediate Steps if Affected by Sepsis

▼
  • Consult a Doctor Nearby: Seek immediate medical attention from the nearest healthcare provider.
  • Immediate Assessment and Diagnosis: Conduct diagnostic tests such as X-rays and CT scans to locate the infection source and assess organ involvement.
  • Analyzing Symptoms: Monitor key symptoms including fever, heart rate, and oxygen saturation levels to evaluate the severity of the condition.
  • Source Control: Remove the source of infection through procedures like draining abscesses, removing infected devices (e.g., catheters), or performing surgical interventions if necessary.
  • Organ Support: Implement organ support measures such as dialysis if the kidneys are failing, along with other interventions to support affected organs.
  • Monitoring and Follow-Up: Continuously monitor vital signs, blood work, and organ function to assess response to treatment and adjust care as needed.
  • Rehabilitation: Once stabilized, patients may require physical therapy, occupational therapy, and psychological support to recover from the effects of sepsis and prolonged hospitalization.
